Great Expectations

Chapter III: The Two Fugitives

It was a miserably raw day, and the mist was heavy. I stole along, seeing nothing but the marshes, and hearing nothing but the wind, until I came near the battery.
I found the man shivering, and he fell upon the food like a dog. He ate the pork-pie, and the bread and butter, and drank the brandy, all in great haste. He then took the file and began to use it on his leg-iron, with his eyes rolling fearfully.
“You young dog,” said he, with a grim smile, “what else is there?”
“Only the mince-pie and the cheese, sir, and the brandy.”
He started suddenly. “Hark! What was that?”
We both listened. There was a faint sound of firing in the distance. The convict became intensely agitated. He finished filing, and his iron was loose.
As he prepared to leave, I ventured to speak of the "young woman" he had threatened me with. He looked surprised and confused, as if he knew nothing of her. This puzzled me greatly.
Then, as I hurried away, I saw another convict, a man with a badly bruised face and a broad-brimmed hat, crouching down in a ditch. He seemed to recognize me and started at the sight of me, but then disappeared.
I fled home, completely exhausted. I told myself I was safe, yet I was more guilty than ever, having served two escaped criminals instead of one.
Just as I reached the door, I heard the sound of footsteps and voices. A party of soldiers, marching with muskets, stopped at the forge. An officer asked Joe if he was the blacksmith.
Joe, surprised, said he was. The officer then asked Joe to mend a broken pair of handcuffs for them. It was Christmas morning.
